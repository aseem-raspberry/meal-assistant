# Users, Jobs & Archetypes
## Understanding Who We Design For and What Progress They Seek

**Version:** 1.0

**Status:** Draft

**Consolidates:** Chapters 4 & 5

---

# 1. The Core Job

People do not want meal recommendations. They want **relief from deciding**.

The product's primary job:

> **Help my household confidently decide what to cook next with the least possible mental effort.**

Key words: Help. Confidently. Household. Least effort.

---

# 2. Three Layers of Jobs

### Functional Job
- What should we cook today?
- What can I make with what's available?
- What can I pack for tomorrow's lunch?
- What can I cook in twenty minutes?
- What can I make from leftovers?

### Emotional Job
- "I don't want to think today."
- "I don't want complaints."
- "I don't want food to go waste."
- "I don't want to disappoint my family."

### Social Job
- "I want the children to enjoy dinner."
- "I want guests to be impressed."
- "I want my parents to feel cared for."
- "I don't want my family to think I always cook the same things."

A recommendation engine that ignores the emotional and social layers will never feel human.

---

# 3. Hidden Jobs

Many important jobs are never explicitly stated:

Avoid wasting vegetables. Use leftovers creatively. Finish ingredients before they expire. Balance nutrition over the week. Reduce grocery expenses. Avoid unnecessary shopping. Reduce arguments. Maintain variety.

These hidden jobs may eventually become stronger differentiators than the primary job.

---

# 4. Current Solutions and Why They Fail

| Solution | Advantage | Failure Mode |
|----------|-----------|-------------|
| Memory | Fast, familiar | High cognitive load, forgets constraints |
| Asking family | Human advice | "I don't know" / "Anything" — transfers the problem |
| WhatsApp relatives | Personalized | Slow, not contextual |
| YouTube | Large variety | Too many choices, increases decision fatigue |
| Recipe websites | Great for learning | Poor for deciding |
| Food delivery | Avoids decision entirely | Indirect competitor, expensive |

**None optimize the complete household decision.** Our opportunity lies in integrating all considerations into one recommendation.

# 6. Jobs Across the Week

Monday: Simple. Tuesday: Routine. Wednesday: Vegetable replenishment. Friday: Lighter workload anticipated. Saturday: Experimentation. Sunday: Family meals.

These rhythms differ across households. The AI should learn them.

---

# 7. What Is a Household?

> A group of people whose meal decisions are interconnected.

This definition intentionally avoids assumptions about family structure. Examples: married couple, parents with children, joint family, shared apartment, elderly couple, household with domestic cook, single individual.

The common characteristic is that meals are planned collectively rather than independently.

---

# 8. Household Dimensions

Instead of demographics, we classify households using **behavioural dimensions**:

| Dimension | Range |
|-----------|-------|
| Household Size | Individual → Couple → Small family → Joint family |
| Decision Maker | One person / Shared / Domestic cook / Elder / Rotational |
| Cooking Frequency | Three fresh daily → Dinner only → Weekend cooking → Batch |
| Planning Style | Highly planned → Semi-planned → Completely spontaneous |
| Experimentation | Traditional → Moderate → Adventurous |
| Budget Sensitivity | Low → Medium → High |
| Health Orientation | Convenience first → Balanced → Highly health conscious |

These dimensions are far more predictive than age or income.

---

# 9. Household Archetypes

### Traditional Multi-Generation Household
Large household, daily fresh cooking, strong cultural traditions, high festival importance. **Challenge:** everyone has different preferences. **Opportunity:** household rules, festival planning, preference balancing, leftover optimization.

### Dual-Income Nuclear Family
Both adults work, time limited. **Challenge:** decision fatigue after work, need for lunchboxes. **Opportunity:** quick mode, tomorrow reminders, meal prep, voice interaction.

### Household With Young Children
Children significantly influence choices; acceptance > variety. **Challenge:** picky eaters, nutrition balance. **Opportunity:** hidden vegetable recipes, lunchbox suggestions, acceptance prediction.

### Elderly Household
Simple cooking, repetitive, digestibility matters. **Challenge:** nutrition, health restrictions. **Opportunity:** simplified recommendations, voice interface, easy shopping lists.

### Shared Apartment
Responsibilities rotate, budgets shared. **Challenge:** who cooks today? Fairness. **Opportunity:** shared accounts, collaborative shopping, cooking assignments.

### Domestic Cook Managed Household
Family decides, cook executes. **Challenge:** communication, inventory awareness. **Opportunity:** daily cooking plan, shareable recommendations, printable meal cards.

### Newly Married Couple
Learning routines, frequent experimentation. **Challenge:** planning, learning, budget. **Opportunity:** guided planning, shopping assistance, learning recommendations.

### Single Professional
Minimal cooking, frequent delivery. **Challenge:** food waste, motivation. **Opportunity:** small quantity recipes, weekly planning, minimal grocery recommendations.

# 10. Household Personalities

Two households with identical demographics may behave completely differently:

- **Explorer:** Enjoys new dishes. High novelty. Low repetition tolerance.
- **Conservative:** Prefers familiar food. High predictability.
- **Efficiency Focused:** Values speed and simplicity. Accepts repetition.
- **Health Optimizer:** Actively balances nutrition. Accepts healthier alternatives.
- **Budget Optimizer:** Prefers affordable meals. Plans shopping carefully.

These personalities may evolve over time and should be inferred, not asked.

---

# 11. Household Evolution

Households are dynamic:

```
Newly married → Young children → Teenagers → Empty nest → Retired couple
```

The product should adapt naturally without forcing users to rebuild profiles.

---

# 12. Decision Makers and Roles

The cook is not always the decision maker. Possible combinations: Mother decides / Father cooks. Grandmother plans / Domestic cook executes. Weekend cooking by spouse.

Potential roles: Primary Decision Maker, Primary Cook, Secondary Cook, Shopper, Inventory Manager, Family Member, Guest.

These roles influence permissions, recommendations, and notifications.

---

# 13. Detecting Archetypes Automatically

Rather than asking, the application should infer archetypes from: household size, meal frequency, shopping behavior, cooking duration, time of cooking, acceptance patterns, inventory size, festival behavior.

Long-term, every household receives a continuously evolving profile:

```
Traditional: 82%    Explorer: 21%       Budget Sensitive: 63%
Health Conscious: 74%    Quick Meals: 81%    Child Acceptance Priority: 92%
```

# 14. Desired Outcomes

If the product succeeds, users experience:

- "I didn't have to think."
- "We used everything in the fridge."
- "The kids actually enjoyed dinner."
- "We haven't wasted vegetables this week."
- "I haven't asked 'What should we cook?' in days."

# 16. Risks

Avoid stereotyping. Avoid assuming gender roles. Avoid assuming dietary preferences based on geography. Avoid assuming income from household size.

Recommendations should emerge from observed behavior rather than preconceived assumptions.

# Decisions Captured

| ID | Decision |
|----|----------|
| D-014 | The product exists to reduce decision fatigue rather than increase recipe discovery. |
| D-015 | Confidence is the product's primary value proposition. |
| D-016 | Every proposed feature should be evaluated by: "Does this help the household make a better food decision?" |
| D-017 | Feature prioritization should be based on job completion rather than novelty. |
| D-018 | All personalization should be household-centric by default. |
| D-019 | The product should infer household archetypes rather than asking users to select one. |
| D-020 | Recommendation algorithms should adapt to evolving household behavior. |
| D-021 | User roles should be modeled separately from household roles. |
