### The Product Constitution

**Version:** 0.1

# 1. Executive Summary

The Household Meal Decision Assistant is an AI-powered decision support system that helps households answer one recurring question:

> **"What should we cook next?"**

Unlike recipe apps, meal planners, calorie trackers or grocery apps, this product focuses on reducing the daily cognitive burden of meal planning.

Its primary objective is not to teach cooking.

Its primary objective is to remove decision fatigue.

The application continuously learns how a household eats, shops, cooks and plans meals until recommendations begin to feel natural.

The long-term vision is to build a digital representation of every household's food behaviour, referred to throughout this document as the **Household Twin**.

---

# 2. Product Vision

Every household deserves an AI assistant that understands its cooking habits as well as the person who usually plans meals.

The application should gradually evolve from a recommendation engine into a trusted household companion that understands:

- Family preferences
- Household traditions
- Weekly rhythm
- Seasonal behaviour
- Inventory
- Leftovers
- Shopping habits
- Cooking effort
- Budget preferences
- Festivals
- Future meal planning

The application should eventually know what a household is likely to cook before the household itself consciously decides.

---

# 3. The Problem We Are Solving

The biggest problem is not cooking.

The biggest problem is deciding.

Every day millions of households repeatedly ask

> "What should we cook today?"

The answer depends on dozens of variables:

- Available ingredients
- Family preferences
- Budget
- Time
- Weather
- Leftovers
- Dietary restrictions
- Previous meals
- Upcoming meals
- Festival traditions
- Household mood

The human brain solves this manually.

Our product should assist that process.

# 4.1 Recipe Content Strategy

Although the product is not a recipe application, recommendations are unusable without execution knowledge.

Recipes are a necessary supporting layer.

They are not the product.

But they must exist.

The strategy is as follows.

## Recipes as Infrastructure

Recipes serve the recommendation engine.

They are never the primary surface.

The user should see "Rajma Chawal" as a recommendation, not as a recipe card.

## Content Sourcing

Phase 1

AI-generated recipes based on dish names and regional cooking styles.

Phase 2

Curated home-style recipes reviewed for authenticity.

Phase 3

Community-contributed household recipes.

Phase 4

Externally linked recipes from trusted platforms.

## Recipe Selection

When a dish has multiple recipes, selection should consider

- Household cooking skill.
- Available equipment.
- Time constraints.
- Ingredient availability.
- Regional preference.

## Principle

Recipes are implementation details that support dishes.

Dishes support meals.

Meals support decisions.

The user should never feel like they are browsing a recipe database.

*Related: D-029 — Recipes are implementation details supporting dishes.*

## Principle 8

Distinguish static context from dynamic context.

Static context can be learned over time through observation.

Examples: preferences, restrictions, household rhythm, seasonal behaviour.

Dynamic context changes per meal occasion and cannot be observed.

Examples: who is eating tonight, available time, unexpected guests, today's mood.

The AI should learn static context passively.

Dynamic context requires lightweight daily capture.

This is not a form.

It is a 10-second micro-interaction.

---

## Principle 9

Progressive Explanation.

The default recommendation should show a single-line rationale.

Example: "Rajma — rainy weather, soaked yesterday."

Full reasoning should be available on demand.

Explanation should reduce cognitive load, never increase it.

The depth of explanation should adapt to the user's pattern:

- New users: show more explanation to build trust.
- Experienced users: show less explanation to reduce noise.
- Rejected recommendations: always show full reasoning.

# 7. Recommendation Philosophy

Recommendations should answer

Why this?

Why today?

Why not yesterday?

Why not something else?

Every recommendation should include

- Primary meal
- Supporting dishes
- Estimated effort
- Reasoning
- Confidence
- Alternatives
- Future meal impact

Recommendations should feel like advice from an experienced family member.

# 8. The Learning Loop

The product improves through continuous feedback.

```
Recommend

↓

Cook

↓

Observe

↓

Learn

↓

Recommend Better
```

Signals include

- Cooked meals
- Meal photographs
- Voice conversations
- Thumbs up
- Thumbs down
- Ignored suggestions
- Inventory updates
- Grocery shopping
- Leftover usage

The objective is to learn passively wherever possible.

# 10. Voice First Philosophy

Cooking is a hands-busy activity.

Voice is therefore a primary interaction model.

Users should naturally ask questions like

"What should I cook?"

"What can I make with potatoes?"

"What can I pack tomorrow?"

"What can I make from yesterday's dal?"

The conversation should feel natural rather than command based.

---

# 11. Cold Start Philosophy

The product must demonstrate value immediately.

The first recommendation should appear within minutes of installation.

Learning should happen gradually.

Users should never feel they are filling out long questionnaires.

The application earns information through usefulness.

---

# 12. Explanation Philosophy

Every recommendation should be explainable.

Example

"We recommended Palak Paneer because:

• Spinach should be used today.

• Paneer hasn't been cooked in 16 days.

• Rainy weather generally matches your comfort-food pattern.

• It pairs well with the leftover dal."

Explainability creates trust.

# 17. Market Scope & Localization Strategy

The product is **India-first**.

All initial design, examples, personas, cuisine models, and festival calendars assume Indian households.

This is intentional.

Indian households represent one of the highest-complexity meal decision environments globally:

- Multi-generational households.
- Extremely high cuisine diversity across regions.
- Daily fresh cooking culture.
- Complex religious and seasonal food rules.
- Leftover transformation culture.
- Domestic cook workflows.

If the product succeeds in this environment, it can generalize.

## Localization Architecture

The following concepts must remain culture-agnostic at the domain model level:

- Household, Member, Meal, Dish, Recipe, Ingredient, Inventory.
- Recommendation, Confidence, Feedback, Household Twin.

The following concepts are inherently culture-specific and must be modelled as pluggable modules:

- Meal component templates (thali vs. bowl meal vs. bread-sabzi).
- Festival calendars.
- Regional cuisine profiles.
- Default ingredient taxonomies.
- Fasting and restriction patterns.

## Expansion Sequence (Illustrative)

Phase 1: Indian households (Hindi, English).

Phase 2: Indian diaspora (US, UK, Middle East).

Phase 3: South-East Asian households.

Phase 4: Global.

Each expansion should validate whether the Household Twin model transfers across cultures or requires fundamental changes.

# 19. Guiding Questions

Whenever we discuss a future feature, we should ask:

1. Does this reduce decision fatigue?

2. Does this improve recommendations?

3. Does this help the Household Twin learn?

4. Does this reduce user effort?

5. Can the AI infer this instead of asking?

6. Does this respect household traditions?

7. Will this increase daily engagement without becoming intrusive?

8. Does it distinguish static context from dynamic context correctly?

9. Does it respect the progressive explanation model?

If the answer to most of these questions is "no", the feature probably does not belong in the product.
