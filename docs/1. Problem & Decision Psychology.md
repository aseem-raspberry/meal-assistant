# Problem & Decision Psychology
## Understanding What We're Solving and How Households Actually Decide

**Version:** 1.0

**Status:** Draft

**Consolidates:** Chapters 2 & 3

---

# 1. The Daily Question

Almost every household asks the same question daily:

> "What should we cook today?"

This appears trivial. It is not.

Unlike purchasing a television or planning a vacation, this decision occurs daily, often multiple times daily, throughout the entire lifetime of a household. A household making three food decisions every day makes over one thousand decisions every year.

The cooking itself is rarely the exhausting part. The exhausting part is **deciding**.

---

# 2. The Decision Behind the Question

When someone asks "What should we cook?" they are actually asking dozens of hidden questions simultaneously:

- What vegetables are available?
- What ingredients are running out or expiring?
- What was cooked yesterday?
- Has everyone eaten enough leafy vegetables this week?
- Is anyone fasting today?
- Is there enough time?
- Does tomorrow require lunch boxes?
- Will everyone eat this?
- Are guests coming?
- Is the weather hot? Should we avoid heavy food?
- Are there leftovers that should be finished?

Humans compress all of these into a single sentence. Our product must unpack that hidden decision process.

---

# 3. Decision Fatigue

Decision fatigue is a well-studied psychological phenomenon. The more decisions a person makes throughout the day, the more mentally exhausted they become.

Cooking decisions often occur late in the day — after work, school, household chores, and childcare. This is precisely when decision quality naturally declines.

Many families therefore default to familiar dishes. Not because they prefer them, but because they require less thinking.

---

# 4. Why Existing Solutions Fail

Thousands of applications exist in the cooking space. Yet households still ask the same question. Because most products optimize the wrong problem.

| Category | What They Solve | Why It's Insufficient |
|----------|----------------|----------------------|
| Recipe Apps | "How do I cook this?" | Users rarely struggle with this. Most households already know how to cook everyday meals. |
| Video Platforms | Cooking techniques & inspiration | Increases possibilities instead of reducing decisions. Fifty recipes don't answer "What tonight?" |
| Nutrition Apps | Calories, protein, carbs | Households optimize for acceptance, convenience, taste, and budget — not macros. |
| Grocery Apps | Procurement | Not planning. |
| Meal Planners | Future planning | Require users to create plans manually. Many abandon them because maintaining the plan requires continuous effort. |

**The Core Gap:** None attempt to become the household's food decision engine.

---

# 5. Understanding Indian Households

Indian households differ significantly from many western households in ways that influence cooking decisions:

**Shared Meals.** Food is prepared for multiple people. Individual optimization is secondary.

**Diverse Preferences.** Grandparents, parents, and children may all have different preferences. One recommendation must satisfy everyone.

**Religious Constraints.** Many households avoid specific foods on specific days (vegetarian Mondays, no onion/garlic during fasting, festival-specific food). These are recurring patterns, not one-time exceptions.

**Fresh Cooking Culture.** Many Indian households prepare fresh meals daily. Unlike frozen meal cultures, inventory changes continuously.

**High Variety.** Families rotate among dozens of dishes. Avoiding repetition is an important objective.

**Leftover Creativity.** Leftovers are frequently transformed rather than discarded. Rice becomes fried rice. Chapati becomes rolls. Dal becomes paratha stuffing. This behaviour is largely absent from current meal planning applications.

---

# 6. The Primary User

The product should initially optimize for the **Household Decision Maker** — the person who makes meal decisions. This may be a mother, father, grandparent, domestic cook, adult child, or working couple.

The product should think in terms of roles rather than gender.

---

# 7. The Meal Decision Pyramid

Not every factor has equal importance. Some constraints completely eliminate choices. Others merely influence ranking.

```
                Preferences

           Variety & Rotation

          Nutrition & Balance

      Effort & Available Time

      Inventory & Leftovers

 Household Rules & Restrictions
```

The lower levels dominate. It doesn't matter how much everyone likes Paneer if today is a fasting day. Preferences become irrelevant if the required ingredients are unavailable.

This hierarchy should directly influence recommendation scoring.

---

# 8. The Six Stages of a Meal Decision

An experienced cook moves through these stages subconsciously:

### Stage 1: Elimination
Remove impossible options. "No onion today." "Paneer finished." "No deep frying." This stage is binary — a dish is either possible or impossible. The recommendation engine should perform this before ranking.

### Stage 2: Availability
What can actually be cooked today? Vegetables, spices, flour, rice, oil, milk, curd, frozen food, leftovers, prepared dough, cooked rice, prepared chutneys. Even time is an ingredient.

### Stage 3: Repetition Check
Humans avoid repeating **ingredients** rather than recipes. Aloo Gobi followed by Aloo Paratha still feels like "too much potato." The recommendation engine should track ingredient frequency, not merely recipe frequency.

### Stage 4: Family Acceptance
"Will everyone eat this?" This is rarely binary. If Father (9), Mother (9), Grandmother (9) love Bhindi but Son (2) dislikes it — does this eliminate Bhindi? Not necessarily. The engine needs to estimate **household satisfaction**, not individual satisfaction.

### Stage 5: Practical Constraints
Experienced cooks balance effort contextually. Monday evening = quick meal. Sunday afternoon = elaborate. Festival = traditional. Children's exams = predictable comfort food. Users should not need to specify this every day.

### Stage 6: Long-Term Planning
Meals rarely exist independently. Today's dinner influences tomorrow. Cook extra rice → tomorrow becomes Fried Rice. Large batch of Rajma → lunch tomorrow solved. Experienced cooks constantly think one meal ahead. Current apps rarely do.

# 9. Time Horizons

Household planning happens on multiple horizons simultaneously:

- **Immediate:** What should I cook now?
- **Daily:** Breakfast, lunch, dinner coordination.
- **Weekly:** Avoid repetition, balance vegetables, use fresh produce.
- **Monthly:** Budget, shopping, festivals, seasonal vegetables.
- **Yearly:** Summer, monsoon, winter, festivals, school vacations.

Each horizon influences recommendations differently.

---

# 10. Emotional Factors

Cooking is not purely rational. Mood matters.

Rain → Pakoras. Cold weather → Soup. Bad day → Comfort food. Celebration → Dessert. Guests → Something impressive.

Many recommendation systems ignore emotional context. Humans do not.

---

# 11. Household Rhythm

Every household develops a rhythm: heavy breakfast / light dinner, Friday special, Sunday elaborate lunch, monthly restaurant visit, seasonal mango recipes, festival traditions.

The app's objective is not merely learning preferences. It is **learning rhythm**.

---

# 12. Decision Shortcuts

Humans use heuristics, not optimization:

- "If unsure, make Dal."
- "If vegetables are running out, make Mixed Vegetable."
- "If everyone is tired, make Khichdi."
- "If guests arrive unexpectedly, make Pulao."

The AI should learn similar heuristics for each household.

---

# 13. Decision Confidence

Every recommendation should include an internal confidence score. Confidence increases when inventory, preferences, history, and rhythm are known. Confidence decreases with guests, vacations, recent onboarding, or missing inventory.

Lower confidence → more alternatives. Higher confidence → fewer but stronger recommendations.

---

# 14. Why Now?

Several technological developments make this problem more solvable today:

- **Vision Models:** Recognize ingredients from photographs, reducing manual inventory management.
- **Speech Recognition:** Voice interaction is practical. Cooking is a hands-busy activity.
- **Large Language Models:** Natural conversations replace complex forms.
- **Personal AI:** Users increasingly expect software to learn their habits.

---

# 15. Risks

| Risk | Mitigation |
|------|-----------|
| **Cold Start** — No history, generic recommendations | Progressive learning. See *AI, Learning & Onboarding.* |
| **Habit Formation** — Users must return consistently | Immediate value. Low friction. Meaningful notifications. |
| **Trust** — Poor recommendations reduce confidence quickly | Explainability. Alternatives. Feedback loops. |
| **Excessive Data Entry** — Manual inventory is tedious | AI inference. Voice. Photos. Behavioural learning. |
