# AI, Learning & Onboarding
## How the Household Twin Learns From Day One Through Year One

**Version:** 1.0

**Status:** Draft

**Priority:** Critical

**Consolidates:** Chapters 9 & 10

---

# 1. The Core Challenge

The cold start is not a technical problem. It is a **trust problem**.

The AI knows nothing about the household. The household does not yet trust the AI. Generic recommendations don't build trust. Asking too many questions destroys patience.

The onboarding and learning strategy must simultaneously demonstrate value, learn passively, build trust, and avoid friction.

---

# 2. Static Context vs. Dynamic Context

This distinction is critical (Blueprint Principle 8).

| Type | Changes | Examples | How Captured |
|------|---------|----------|-------------|
| **Static** | Slowly (weeks/months) | Preferences, restrictions, cuisine region, cooking frequency, budget orientation | Learned through observation |
| **Dynamic** | Per meal occasion | Who's eating tonight, available time, mood, unexpected guests, leftover availability | Lightweight daily micro-interaction |

Onboarding captures minimal static context. Daily interaction captures dynamic context through a 10-second micro-interaction — not a form.

---

# 3. Minimal Onboarding

The application becomes useful after capturing only:

**Required (Minute One):**
1. Household size.
2. Primary cuisine region.
3. Vegetarian or non-vegetarian.

**Optional (First Week, Progressive):**
4. Allergies or hard restrictions.
5. Cooking frequency.
6. Children present (yes/no).

**Never ask during onboarding:** Individual dish preferences, ingredient likes/dislikes, shopping habits, budget, festival traditions. Learn these through observation.

---

# 4. Day One Recommendation Strategy

Without history, the recommendation engine relies on:

- **Population Priors:** What do similar households commonly cook?
- **Seasonal Defaults:** What's in season? What dishes match this weather?
- **Cultural Defaults:** Universally popular dishes within the cuisine region.
- **Low-Risk Strategy:** Prefer familiar, widely-accepted dishes over adventurous ones.

```
Tonight we suggest: Dal Tadka with Jeera Rice

Why?
• Popular weeknight dinner in your region.
• Uses commonly available ingredients.
• Quick preparation (30 minutes).

Is this close to what your family enjoys?
```

The question at the end simultaneously delivers value and collects feedback.

---

# 5. The First Seven Days

| Day | Focus |
|-----|-------|
| 1 | Minimal onboarding. First recommendation using population priors. |
| 2-3 | Learn from acceptance/rejection. Detect vegetable and effort preferences. |
| 4-5 | First signs of personalization. Avoid repeating ingredients from Days 1-3. |
| 6-7 | Household should notice recommendations are improving. **Critical retention moment.** |

# 6. The Trust Ladder

| Stage | User Feeling | Timeline |
|-------|-------------|----------|
| Plausible | "That's a reasonable suggestion." | Week 1 |
| Relevant | "That actually fits our situation." | Month 1 |
| Impressive | "How did it know we wanted that?" | Month 3 |
| Trusted | "Just tell me what to cook." | Sustained accuracy |

The product should track which stage each household has reached.

---

# 7. Signal Taxonomy

### Tier 1: High-Confidence
Directly reflect household decisions. Meal cooked (strongest positive). Recommendation rejected with reason. Meal photographed. Explicit thumbs up/down.

### Tier 2: Medium-Confidence
Require inference. Recommendation ignored (weak negative). Time between recommendation and decision. Recommendation modified. Shopping patterns.

### Tier 3: Passive
Usage patterns. Time of day app is opened. Day of week patterns. Seasonal changes. Cooking duration. Meal complexity patterns. Notification engagement.

### Tier 4: External
From outside the application. Weather API. Festival calendar. School/holiday calendar. Regional vegetable seasonality.

---

# 8. The Learning Loop

```
Decision Context → Recommendation Generated → Presented → Household Response
  → Context + Response = Training Example → Household Twin Updated
    → Better Context Understanding → Better Recommendation
```

The Decision Context (see *Domain Model* §31) must be stored alongside every Feedback Signal. Without context, the system cannot learn *why* a recommendation succeeded or failed.

---

# 9. What the Household Twin Learns

**Preference Model.** Dynamic per-member and per-household preference scores, accounting for frequency, recency, context, and rejection patterns.

**Rhythm Model.** Temporal patterns: Sunday elaborate, Monday quick, Friday lighter, month-end budget-conscious. Detected over a 4-8 week window.

**Effort Model.** Average cooking duration, dish complexity, day-of-week effort variation.

**Repetition Tolerance.** Household-specific ingredient repetition windows. Some households eat dal daily. Others expect 7+ days between appearances.

**Acceptance Prediction.** Given a proposed meal + participating members → probability of household satisfaction.

**Seasonal Behaviour.** Summer: lighter meals, more curd. Monsoon: comfort food, pakoras. Winter: heavier meals, more ghee. Requires a full year of data.

# 10. The Household Satisfaction Function

Operationally defined:

```
Household Satisfaction = Σ (Member Weight × Member Satisfaction)
                         ─────────────────────────────────────
                         Σ Member Weights

Member Satisfaction = f(Preference Score, Repetition Penalty,
                       Effort Match, Contextual Fit, Seasonal Fit)
```

**Member Weights** are not fixed importance rankings. They are participation-adjusted: a member not eating tonight has weight = 0. A present member's weight is proportional to their historical sensitivity — a child who frequently complains has higher effective weight than a parent who accepts most meals.

This avoids the ethical concern of explicitly ranking family members.

**Veto Detection.** If a member has *never* eaten a particular ingredient in 6+ months, treat it as a hard constraint even without explicit restriction.

---

# 11. AI Confidence vs. User Confidence

These are distinct signals (a critical inconsistency resolved from the original documentation):

| AI Confidence | User Confidence | Recommended Behaviour |
|:---:|:---:|---|
| High | High | One strong recommendation, minimal explanation |
| High | Low | One strong recommendation, detailed explanation |
| Low | High | Show alternatives, respect user's inclination |
| Low | Low | Ask a diagnostic question before recommending |

---

# 12. Learning Speed

| Period | What's Learned |
|--------|---------------|
| Week 1 | Basic diet type, cuisine region, meal frequency |
| Month 1 | Top 20 preferred dishes, major dislikes, weekday/weekend rhythm |
| Quarter 1 | Seasonal patterns, effort preferences, repetition tolerance, individual member acceptance |
| Year 1 | Full Household Twin operational. Predictive recommendations. Seasonal cycle complete. |
| Year 1+ | Refinement. Life stage transitions detected. Long-term preference evolution. |

---

# 13. What the AI Should NOT Learn

**Temporary situations as long-term patterns.** Two weeks of delivery during vacation ≠ preference for delivery. The system should detect "anomaly periods" and quarantine that data.

**Cultural assumptions from demographics.** Never assume preferences based on religion, region, or household structure. All preferences must be observed.

---

# 14. Progressive Profiling

Instead of forms, learn through natural interactions:

**Implicit:** Which recommendations accepted/rejected/modified? What time do they typically interact? How long deciding?

**Lightweight questions** (max one per day): "Was dinner enough for everyone?" / "Did you have leftover dal today?" / "Do you usually cook breakfast at home?"

Each question serves a specific learning objective. No question should feel like a survey.

---

# 15. Cold Start for Specific Entities

**Inventory:** Don't ask for full pantry. Infer from cooked meals — if they cook Rajma, they have Rajma, onions, tomatoes, and spices.

**Leftovers:** Ask once after a meal: "Any leftovers?" — a lightweight dynamic context question.

**Shopping:** Don't model until 15-20 meals cooked. Premature shopping suggestions reduce trust.

---

# 16. Failure Recovery

If the first three recommendations are all rejected:

1. Acknowledge: "I'm still learning your household."
2. Ask one diagnostic question: "What did you end up cooking?"
3. Treat the answer as high-value training data.
4. Adjust population prior weights.

Three consecutive rejections should never silently continue.

# Decisions Captured

| ID | Decision |
|----|----------|
| D-039 | Onboarding captures no more than three required data points: household size, cuisine region, diet type. |
| D-040 | Day One recommendations use population priors and seasonal defaults, not household-specific data. |
| D-041 | Aim for "plausible" within week one; "relevant" within month one. |
| D-042 | Progressive profiling through natural interactions, not forms. |
| D-043 | The AI learns household behaviour, not recipes or nutrition. |
| D-044 | AI Confidence and User Confidence are distinct signals requiring different response strategies. |
| D-045 | Member weights are participation-adjusted and sensitivity-learned, not explicitly ranked. |
| D-046 | Detect anomaly periods (vacations, illness) and quarantine from long-term learning. |
